Sarah
  • National Firearms Act of 1934
    • Taxes the manufacture and transfer of, and mandates the registration of Title II weapons such as machine guns, short-barreled rifles and shotguns, heavy weapons, explosive ordnance, suppressors, and disguised or improvised firearms.
  • Gun Control Act of 1968
    • Regulates the firearms industry and firearms owners. It primarily focuses on regulating interstate commerce in firearms by generally prohibiting interstate firearms transfers except among licensed manufacturers, dealers and importers.
  • Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968
    • Prohibited interstate trade in handguns, increased the minimum age to 21 for buying handguns.
  • Firearm Owners Protection Act 1986
    • Revised and partially repealed the Gun Control Act of 1968. Prohibited the sale to civilians of automatic firearms manufactured after the date of the law's passage. Required ATF approval of transfers of automatic firearms.
  • Undetectable Firearms Act of 1988
    • Effectively criminalizes, with a few exceptions, the manufacture, importation, sale, shipment, delivery, possession, transfer, or receipt of firearms with less than 3.7 oz of metal content.
  • Gun-Free School Zones Act 1990
    • Prohibits unauthorized individuals from knowingly possessing a firearm at a place that the individual knows, or has reasonable cause to believe, is a school zone.

1. About the Issue

Amay

Protecting the environment has become more important to the Americans over the years. With the publication of Silent Spring by Rachel Carson, the public became aware of the impact of the pesticide DDT. As a result, this compound was banned, and eventually the Environmental Protection Agency was created by President Nixon. Since then, the issues around environmental protection and regulation have morphed from dealing with pure science into ones which need to balance the competing needs of keeping people healthy while allowing economic development and prosperity. The key issues facing people today include: (i) Global Warming/Climate Change, (ii) Reduction in harmful pollutants in the air and water, (iii) Managing wildlife to ensure biodiversity. Climate change refers to the impact of rising levels of greenhouse gases (gases that cause the temperature of the atmosphere is rise) in the atmosphere. With increased concentrations of these gases, the overall temperature in the atmosphere increases, and is causing changes in weather patterns and temperatures. While most people think of these changes as causing increased temperatures, they actually are causing increased extreme events, including colder temperatures in some locations and higher temperature in others. Keeping clean air and water for people and animals is another goal of environmental regulation and politics. However, it is not just as simple as stopping pollution. Economic development of a nation causes industries to release waste materials from factories. How much of each substance and how it can be released is the subject of environmental regulation. Sometimes, the regulations cause industries to close down, or force them to switch raw materials. For example, restrictions introduced by President Obama under the clean air act, are forcing Power Plants to consider switching from coal to natural gas. Finally, managing wildlife is another environmental issue. While humans are the largest species on Earth, there are millions of other species that call this plant home. However, many of them live in areas that humans want to develop for their own economic needs. How to balance the needs of the competing interests of human economic development and protection of these species is the role of environmental policy. For example, reintroducing wolves into Yellowstone has enabled the ecosystem to become rebalanced. However at the same time, wolves have been known to attack and kill cattle being raised by ranchers in the area. How to protect important wild species while also protecting the economic rights of people in the area is a difficult balancing act that politicians need to deal with.

Brianna

Full government regulation is the most apparent option to change the worsening state of the environment, through business friendly regulations that still support free trade options. 6. Opposing Facts

Gabi
  • Evidence of climate change - NASA
  • Global Temperature Rise
    • The planet's average surface temperature has risen about 1.62 degrees Fahrenheit (0.9 degrees Celsius) since the late 19th century, a change driven largely by increased carbon dioxide and other human-made emissions into the atmosphere.
  • Warming Oceans
    • The oceans have absorbed much of this increased heat, with the top 700 meters (about 2,300 feet) of ocean showing warming of more than 0.4 degrees Fahrenheit since 1969.
  • Shrinking Ice Sheets
    • The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets have decreased in mass. Data from NASA's Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment show Greenland lost an average of 286 billion tons of ice per year between 1993 and 2016, while Antarctica lost about 127 billion tons of ice per year during the same time period. The rate of Antarctica ice mass loss has tripled in the last decade.
  • Glacial Retreat
    • Glaciers are retreating almost everywhere around the world — including in the Alps, Himalayas, Andes, Rockies, Alaska and Africa.
  • Decreased Snow Cover
    • Satellite observations reveal that the amount of spring snow cover in the Northern Hemisphere has decreased over the past five decades and that the snow is melting earlier.
  • Sea Level Rise
    • Global sea level rose about 8 inches in the last century. The rate in the last two decades, however, is nearly double that of the last century and is accelerating slightly every year.
  • Declining Arctic Sea Ice
    • Both the extent and thickness of Arctic sea ice has declined rapidly over the last several decades.
  • Extreme Events
    • The number of record high temperature events in the United States has been increasing, while the number of record low temperature events has been decreasing, since 1950. The U.S. has also witnessed increasing numbers of intense rainfall events.
  • Ocean Acidification
    • Since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution, the acidity of surface ocean waters has increased by about 30 percent.This increase is the result of humans emitting more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere and hence more being absorbed into the oceans. The amount of carbon dioxide absorbed by the upper layer of the oceans is increasing by about 2 billion tons per year.

    • Supports programs to notify the public of any potential for exposure to diseases in applicable coastal waters
  • Endangered Species Act
    • Requires federal agencies to make sure that any actions they are taking are not going to harm or jeopardize the worldwide list of of endangered species maintained by the U.S Fish and Wildlife Service
    • Prohibits the importation, exportation, or any commerce of the species that are on the list as well
  • Energy Independence and Security Act
    • Greater energy and independence for U.S
    • Increase production of clean renewable fuels
    • Protect consumers
    • Increase efficiency of products, buildings and vehicles
    • Promote research on and plans around greenhouse gas capture and storage
    • Push the Federal Government towards better energy performance
  • Nuclear Waste Policy Act
    • Procedures to evaluate sites that can be used for geologic repositories
    • Supports using deep geologic repositories for safe storage of radioactive waste
  • Safe Drinking Water Act
    • Protect quality of drinking water
    • Allows EPA to establish minimum standards to protect tap water
    • Those who operate public water systems must comply with such standards set by the EPA
  • Pollution Prevention Act
    • Reducing pollution through cost-effective changes in production, operation, and raw materials
      • Equipment for technology modifications
      • Process modifications
      • Redesign of products
      • Substituting raw materials
      • Improving housekeeping, maintenance, training, and inventory control
    • Increasing efficiency in energy and water use
  • Shore Protection Act
    • Prohibits the transportation of commercial waste within coastal waters without a permit
    • EPA responsible for creating regulations concerning loading, securing, offloading, and cleaning up wastes from waste sources, vessels, and reception facilities
    • Goal → minimize deposit of waste in coastal waters and ensure that any waste that is deposited is reported and cleaned up.
